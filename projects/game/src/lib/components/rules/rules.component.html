Object

To put the opponent's king in 'Checkmate'.

Moves

The players take turns moving pieces as described below. The following conditions must be met for a legal move:

  • The player's king may not be in 'Check' at the end of the move.
  • The player must move a piece as described below.

If either condition can not be met, the game is over. If the player's king is in 'Check' it is termed 'Checkmate' and the player loses. If the player's king is not in 'Check', it is termed a 'Statemate' and the game is a tie.

Check

A king is in 'Check' if the hex it occupies is attacked by an opposing piece as described below.

Taking

A piece is taken when an opposing piece moves to occupy it's hex. The taken piece is removed from the board

Pieces

Pawns move directly forward one space if the space is not occupied. Pawns can only attack one space diagonaly forward to either side, they cannot attack directly forward.

Two-space move and 'en passant': If a pawn has not made a previous move in the game, it may move forward two spaces if both spaces are not occupied. If the intermediate space is attacked by an opposing pawn, the opponent has the option of 'taking' the pawn on the next move as if it had only been moved one space. This right is called 'en passant' ('take in passing') and must be exercised immediately or forfeited.

Promoting: When a pawn reaches the rank furthest from its starting position it must be exchanged as part of the same move on the same hex for a new queen, rook, bishop, or knight of the same colour. The player’s choice is not restricted to pieces that have been captured previously. This exchange of a pawn for another piece is called ‘promotion’ and the effect of the new piece is immediate.

Knights both move and attack in an L shape, either two spaces vertically and one horizontally, or one vertically and two horizontally. Knights can jump over any piece (though any jumped pieces are not removed) and therefore cannot be blocked.

Bishops both move and attack any number of spaces diagonaly in a straight line. They may may not jump pieces in their path and can therefore be blocked.

Rooks can both move and attack any number of spaces horizontaly or vertically in a straight line. They may not jump pieces in their path and can therefore be blocked.

Queens both move and attack any number of spaces in any direction in a straight line. They may not jump pieces in their path and can therefore be blocked.

Kings both move and attack one space in any direction.

Dark McCooey Hex Chess

Dark McCooey Hex Chess is a variant of McCooey Hex Chess. All the pieces move in the same fashion. The differences are:

  • A player is only able to see hexes that their pieces are capable of moving to or attacking, hence the name Dark McCooey Hex Chess.
  • There is no concept of 'check'. Because of the above limitation, a king may mot be able to see all of it's attackers. A player may make a move that results in their king being taken on the opponent's next move.
  • There is no concept of 'stalemate' because there is no check.
  • There is no concept of 'checkmate' because there is no check.
  • Since there is no concept of 'check' or 'checkmate', the object of the game is to capture the opponent's king.
